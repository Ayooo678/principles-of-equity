What Is Equity? ⚖️

Equity is that body of rules developed originally by the Court of Chancery — created to remedy the harshness of Common Law, give effect to the intentions of parties, and prevent unconscionable conduct. It is not mere fairness in the abstract; it is a systematic body of law with its own doctrines, maxims, cases, and remedies.

This course covers the core doctrines and equitable remedies — from the historical development of Chancery to specific performance, injunctions, conversion, assignment, rescission, rectification, and the great equitable defences. Each unit is built around real Nigerian and English cases, illustrative hypotheticals, and plain-English explanations that make the law stick.

Unit 1

History & Development of Equity

How a bunch of overworked petitioners accidentally created an entire legal system.

What Even Is Equity? 🤔

To your neighbour on the street: "equity = fairness, honesty, doing right by people." Cute. But legally useless.

Legally, equity has two meanings:

  • Technical meaning: A special legal system developed by the Court of Chancery in England to fix the harshness of Common Law.
  • Broad meaning: The power of a court to apply moral principles and do substantial justice — adapting relief to the specific circumstances of each case.
💡
Take-Away Note

"Equity is that body of rules administered only by Courts of Equity." This definition worked before the Judicature Act 1875 fused everything together. After 1875? One court, two systems, running side by side.

The Timeline of Drama 🎭

  • 1066 — Norman Conquest: William the Conqueror arrives. A unified "Common Law" starts developing through the King's Courts.
  • Early days: If you wanted relief, you needed a royal writ from the Chancellor's office. No writ = no case.
  • Problem: Common Law became rigid. If your grievance didn't fit an existing writ, you were finished.
  • 1258 — Provisions of Oxford: Parliament panicked and said "no new writs without the King's Council's consent." Innovation: frozen.
  • 1285 — Statute of Westminster II (in consimili casu): A small thaw — clerks could modify existing writs for similar cases.
  • Still problems: Common Law judges quashed any writ that looked "too new." Injustice continued.
  • The Petition Era: People gave up on courts and started petitioning the King directly. The King passed them all to the Chancellor.
  • The Chancellors: Early ones were clergymen who decided cases based on conscience, not precedent.
🎲 Quick Hypothetical

Imagine a mortgage law that says: "Pay back in exactly 3 months or lose your land forever." Chidi paid back on Day 92 — two days late. Common Law said: "Too bad, property gone." Equity said: "Wait, that's unconscionable — Chidi, come to the Chancellor." That's why equity exists.

John Selden's Famous Shade ☕

"Equity is a roguish thing… it is according to the conscience of the Chancellor, and as that is larger or narrower, so is equity. As if they should make the standard for the measure of a Chancellor's foot."

Translation: "Every Chancellor has a different-sized conscience and that's chaotic." And he wasn't wrong — yet.

THE BIG CONFLICT ⚔️
🏠
Earl of Oxford's Case (1615)The Case That Changed Everything — Locus Classicus
⭐ Locus Classicus
📖 The Scenario

Emeka takes a lease, builds a house, plants a garden — all based on a contract with an option to buy. The landlord forcefully evicts him. Common Law: "Landlord wins." Chancery grants an injunction. Lord Coke loses his mind. Lord Ellesmere: "He who builds a house must live in it. Good conscience demands we protect Emeka."

⚖️ Resolution

King James I sided with Chancery. From that point forward: where Equity and Common Law conflict, EQUITY PREVAILS. Later codified in the Judicature Act 1873.

🏆
Key Statutes

Judicature Act 1873 Judicature Act 1875 — One Supreme Court. Both systems applied by all divisions. Where they clash? Equity wins.

Era of Systematization ⚙️

When lawyers (not clergymen) became Chancellors, conscience gave way to precedent. Lord Nottingham transformed equity from vibes-based justice into a systematic body of rules. Lord Eldon: "Nothing will inflict greater pain on me than the recollection that I had done anything to justify reproach that the equity of this court varies like the Chancellor's foot."

🏭
Walsh v Lonsdale (1882)Equity Treats Agreement as the Real Thing
📖 The Scenario

No formal deed for a 7-year mill lease was ever signed. Lonsdale demanded rent in advance (as the agreement allowed). Walsh refused: "No deed = no legal lease = not bound by advance rent."

⚖️ Held

Equity treats an enforceable written agreement for a lease as good as the lease itself — equity looks on as done that which ought to be done. Walsh was bound by the advance rent term.

Reception of Equity in Nigeria 🇳🇬

The High Court Laws of various Nigerian states provide that Law and Equity shall be administered concurrently. Section 45(1), Interpretation Act also supports this.

Unit 2

The 12 Maxims of Equity

The moral compass of the Court of Chancery. Remember: in exams, don't number them — state them by name. You've been warned.

Ubi jus, ibi remedium

1. Equity Will Not Suffer a Wrong to Be Without a Remedy

The very root of equitable jurisdiction. Where Common Law fails, Equity steps in.

Operates in three jurisdictions:

Original Jurisdiction

Equity created entirely new rights. Example: Trusts. At Common Law, only the trustee's legal title mattered. Equity said: "The trustee holds the property FOR the beneficiaries."

Concurrent Jurisdiction

Both systems gave a remedy, but Common Law's was inadequate. Example: breach of contract → Common Law gives only damages; Equity also gives specific performance or an injunction.

Auxiliary Jurisdiction

Equity helped proceedings along by ordering things Common Law couldn't — like discovery of documents.

Aequitas sequitur legem

2. Equity Follows the Law

Equity supplements — it doesn't replace. Equity will not allow a statute to be used as an instrument of fraud.

💬
Key Quote — Trans Bridge Co. Ltd v Survey International Ltd (1986)

Kayode Eso JSC: "Equity is not a warlord, determined to do battle with the law. It is part of a legal system mixed with the law for the purpose of achieving justice."

3. Where There is Equal Equity, the Law Shall Prevail

Legal interest beats equitable interest — but only when their moral claims are equally clean.

🔍
Pilcher v Rawlins (1872)The Golden Rule — Locus Classicus
⭐ Locus Classicus
📖 The Scenario

A fraudulent trustee concealed a mortgage and sold the legal estate to honest purchasers who paid full price with no notice. The trust beneficiaries tried to claim the land.

⚖️ Held

Bona fide purchasers for value without notice take free of prior equitable interests. James LJ: "an absolute, unqualified, unanswerable defence."

Qui prior est tempore, potior est jure

4. Where Equities are Equal, the First in Time Prevails

Among competing equitable interests of equal moral standing, whoever got there first wins.

📬
Dearle v HallException for Pure Personalty — Notice First Wins
📖 The Scenario

Taiwo assigns his trust fund interest to Ada (first), then to Bode (second). Ada never notifies the trustees. Bode notifies immediately.

⚖️ Held

For equitable interests in pure personalty, priority goes to whoever gives first notice to the trustees — not whoever created their interest first.

5. He Who Seeks Equity Must Do Equity

You want equity to help you? Then be prepared to act fairly towards the other side going forward. This maxim is about future conduct.

6. He Who Comes to Equity Must Come With Clean Hands

This is about your PAST behaviour. Unclean conduct in THIS transaction disqualifies you from relief.

🆚
Maxim 5 vs Maxim 6

Maxim 5 = what you must be WILLING TO DO in the future to get relief.
Maxim 6 = what you have ALREADY DONE that might disqualify you. Gill v Lewis: the misconduct must be directly connected to the breach in question.

Vigilantibus non dormientibus jura subveniunt

7. Delay Defeats Equities (Laches)

Equity aids the vigilant, not the lazy. Limitation periods: contracts 6yrs, land 12yrs, public land 20yrs.

8. Equality is Equity

Where no fair basis for unequal distribution exists, equity divides equally. Equity prefers tenancy in common over joint tenancy — no jus accrescendi in commerce.

9. Equity Looks on the Intent (Substance) Rather Than the Form

The label on a document doesn't fool equity. Equity X-rays the transaction to find its real nature.

10. Equity Looks on That as Done Which Ought to Be Done

If you're legally obliged to do something, equity treats it as already done. Walsh v Lonsdale; Iragunima v Rivers State Housing Authority.

11. Equity Imputes an Intention to Fulfill an Obligation

If you do Y (which fulfills obligation X), equity treats Y as your fulfillment. Only between spouses and parent/child. Equity does not perfect imperfect gifts.

12. Equity Acts In Personam

Equitable orders are directed at a PERSON's conscience — not at the property itself. This gives equity global reach.

🌍
Ayinule v Abimbola (1957)Injunction Across Borders
📖 The Scenario

The plaintiff discovered the defendant was using his registered trade name in Ghana. He sued in Lagos for an injunction. The defendant argued Ghana was outside the court's jurisdiction.

⚖️ Held

An injunction is an order in personam — directed at the defendant personally. As long as the defendant is within the court's reach, the court can restrain their conduct anywhere. Injunction granted.

🏆
Root Maxims Alert

Maxims 1 and 12 are the root and foundation of equitable jurisdiction everywhere. Everything flows from: (1) "equity will remedy a wrong" and (12) "equity does so by acting on the person."

Unit 3

Doctrine of Priority

When multiple people claim the same land and it's not worth enough to pay everyone — who gets paid first?

When Does Priority Arise? 🤔

  • Multiple Mortgages: An owner uses the same land as security to borrow from different lenders. He defaults. The land can't pay everyone.
  • Multiple Sales: The same owner sells the same piece of land to different people at different times.

The General Rule: First in Time, First in Right ⏰

Qui prior est tempore, potior est jure — he who is earlier in time is stronger in law. Applies to both legal and equitable interests (subject to exceptions).

📋
Tewogbade v Obadina (1994)Nemo Dat in Action
📖 The Scenario

Obadina had a deed of conveyance dated September 1968, registered May 1969. Tewogbade had a deed dated November 1975. Same seller. Same land.

⚖️ Held

Obadina wins. Nemo dat quod non habet — you cannot give what you do not have. The 1975 conveyance was void.

Four Limits That Can Flip Priority 🔄

  1. Legal beats equitable (equal equity required)
  2. Unequal equities — prior claimant's conduct caused the problem
  3. Fraud and gross negligence (Northern Counties Fire Insurance v Whipp)
  4. Bona fide purchaser for value without notice

The Ultimate Shield — Bona Fide Purchaser 🛡️

Three requirements: (1) Bona fide — acted honestly, no knowledge of prior equity; (2) Purchaser for value — money, money's worth, marriage consideration, or satisfaction of existing debt; (3) Without notice — no actual, constructive, or imputed notice at the time of giving consideration, plus obtained the legal estate.

🏠
Animashaun v Olojo (1990)Bona Fide Defence Fails — Constructive Notice — Locus Classicus
⭐ Locus Classicus on Constructive Notice
📖 The Scenario

Executors sold a plot to the Respondent in 1975 (receipt + immediate possession, no deed). Same executors sold the same plot to Animashaun in 1976 with a registered deed. But Animashaun NEVER visited the land before buying. When she arrived to take possession, the Respondent was already there.

⚖️ Held

Animashaun had constructive notice — a reasonable purchaser would have inspected the land. She was not a bona fide purchaser. The Respondent's prior equitable interest prevails.

Unit 4

Doctrine of Notice

Did you know? Did you ought to have known? Or did your lawyer know and forget to tell you? Three questions. Three types of notice.

👀

Actual Notice

You personally knew about the prior interest at the time of the transaction.

🔍

Constructive Notice

You didn't personally know, but you WOULD have known if you'd made proper inquiries.

🤝

Imputed Notice

Your agent (e.g. your lawyer) knew about it, so the law treats it as YOUR knowledge.

Actual Notice 👀

You have actual notice of all facts you actually know — however you came to know them. Includes knowledge acquired any time before completion of the transaction.

👂
Williamson v Brown (1875)The "Prudent Man" Test — Locus Classicus on Constructive Notice
⭐ Locus Classicus
📖 The Scenario

Williamson heard community rumours that Brown held a mortgage over the land he was buying. He checked the official records — nothing. He bought, dismissing the rumours.

⚖️ Held (Selden J)

"Where a purchaser has knowledge of ANY fact sufficient to put him on inquiry, he is presumed either to have made the inquiry and discovered the truth — or to have been guilty of negligence equal to actual knowledge." The rumours were enough. Constructive notice applied.

Imputed Notice 🤝

When you engage an agent to handle a property transaction, any actual or constructive notice the agent receives in the course of that transaction is imputed to you — even if the agent fails to tell you. Notice to agent = notice to principal. EXCEPTION: knowledge from a previous, unrelated transaction cannot be imputed in a later transaction (Okunubi v Assaf).

Unit 5

Land Registration & Priority

In Nigeria, registration of land instruments changes the priority game. He who registers first — wins.

The Golden Rule of Priority Through Registration ⭐

Priority among registrable instruments is determined NOT by when they were created, but by when they were PRESENTED for registration. The date and hour of registration govern everything.

⚖️
Orianzi v AG Rivers State (2017)Augie JSC States the Rule Clearly
📖 Augie JSC Stated

"Priority as regards registrable instruments is determined by the dates of registration and not by the dates the instrument was made… What counts is the date and hour of registration."

⚖️ Practical Example

If four legal mortgages are created over the same land and the LAST one registers first — that last mortgagee gets priority over all three others. Registration date = everything.

📅
Ayinla v Sijuwola (1984)First to Register Wins — But Registration Doesn't Cure Defects
📖 The Scenario

Both parties purchased the same piece of land from the same vendor and both registered. Sijuwola registered first.

⚖️ Held

Sijuwola's earlier registration takes priority. BUT — registration does not cure a defective title. Once the vendor sold to Sijuwola, he had nothing left to sell Ayinla. Ayinla's deed was void regardless of registration (nemo dat).

Registration and Notice 🔔

Registration constitutes constructive notice to all subsequent purchasers. An unregistered instrument cannot prove title — but CAN prove payment + equitable interest (especially combined with possession).

Unit 6

Assignment of Choses in Action

Transferring intangible rights from one person to another — legally, equitably, or barely at all.

What Is an Assignment? 🤝

An assignment is the transfer of the totality of interest in things or choses in action from an assignor (A) to an assignee (B). In FCMB v. Essien, defined as "a transfer or setting over of property or some right or interest in the property from one person to another." Assignment does NOT require the debtor's consent. Novation requires the consent of ALL parties — including the debtor (NNPC v. Clifco Nig. Ltd, 2011).

Chose in Action 📚

In Torkington v. Magee: "a personal right in property which can only be claimed or enforced by action, and not by taking physical possession." Two types: Pure Intangibles (debts, IP — right exists independently of any document) and Documentary Intangibles (bills of exchange, shares — the right IS the document). Choses can be legal, equitable, or future.

NAWA NI — Your Exam Acronym

Legal Assignment: Notice · Assignment · Writing · Absolute · No Consideration · Identity

All six must be present for a valid legal assignment under s.25(6) Judicature Act or s.150 PCL.

💸
Julius Berger Nig. Plc v. Toki Rainbow Community Bank (2019)Direction vs. Assignment — Locus Classicus
⭐ Locus Classicus
📖 The Scenario

Pit-a-Pat borrowed from Toki Rainbow Bank and wrote letters directing Julius Berger to pay contract proceeds directly to the Bank. When Pit-a-Pat defaulted, the Bank sued Julius Berger claiming the letters were an assignment.

⚖️ Held (Supreme Court)

The letters were a domiciliation arrangement — not a legal or equitable assignment. No assignment of benefits = no right for the Bank to sue Julius Berger.

Equitable Assignment 🌿

Requires only a clear intention to transfer — oral, written, or from conduct. In Re McArdle (1951): "Equity looks into the intent rather than the form." Two types: (1) By way of contract; (2) By way of trust (four variants: declaration of self as trustee, transfer on trust, direction to trustees, creation of sub-trust). Exceptions requiring writing: subsisting trusts (Grey v. IRC) and copyright/patents.

🏆
Dearle v. Hall (1823)Notice = Priority Among Competing Assignees
⭐ Locus Classicus
📖 The Rule

Zechariah Brown assigned his trust interest to Dearle (1808), Sherring (1809), then Hall (1812). Hall investigated, found no prior dealings, and gave immediate written notice to the executors. Executors later received notice of the earlier assignments.

⚖️ Held

Priority between successive equitable assignees is determined by who gives FIRST notice to the trustee/debtor — not by who created their interest first.

Unit 7

Doctrine of Conversion

Equity's magical trick — turning land into money and money into land, before anyone actually does anything.

What Is Conversion? 🔄

Conversion is equity's invention of treating land as money and money as land from the moment the owner expresses the intention that such should be the case. The actual conversion doesn't need to have happened. Based on: "Equity regards as done that which ought to be done." Under a Deed: from the date of execution. Under a Will: from the date of the testator's death.

🌳
Fletcher v. AshburnerThe Classic Statement — Locus Classicus
⭐ Locus Classicus
📖 Sir Thomas Sewell MR

"Nothing was better established than this principle: that money directed to be employed in the purchase of land, and land directed to be sold and turned into money, are to be considered as that species of property into which they are directed to be converted."

The HIID Rules — When Conversion WON'T Apply 🚦

H — Hardship

Conversion will not be ordered where it creates hardship on the defendant, especially due to subsequent changes not contemplated at contracting. Clay v. Landreth.

I — Intention

The doctrine only applies if the intention of the parties showed that conversion should take effect.

I — Inequitable Results

Application is limited to cases where it will NOT produce inequitable results.

D — Discretion

Conversion is applied at the discretion of the court. It is not a matter of right.

6 CIRCUMSTANCES WHERE CONVERSION APPLIES 📋

1–6 Quick Summary

1. Binding Contract for Sale/Purchase of Land

From the moment a binding, enforceable contract is signed, land becomes money and money becomes land in equity. The vendor holds on trust for the purchaser (Daire v. Beversham; Seton v. Slade).

2. Option to Purchase — Rule in Lawes v. Bennett

When an option to purchase is exercised after the grantor's death, the purchase price is treated as personalty — relating back to the date the option was originally granted.

3. Trust for Sale or Purchase

Where a will directs land to be sold, it is immediately treated as money from the date of death. The direction must be IMPERATIVE — not optional (Curling v. May: discretionary direction = no conversion).

4. Statutory Trust for Sale

Under Sections 61–64 PCL 1959, land held in undivided shares is automatically subject to a statutory trust for sale — causing automatic conversion into personalty (Re Kempthrone).

5. Partnership Property

Land owned in a partnership is treated as personalty — because on dissolution it will be sold and divided. s.21, Partnership Law (Lagos State).

6. Court Order

Where a court orders the sale of real property, conversion is operative from the date of the order — not the date of actual sale (Fauntleroy v. Beebe).

Failure & Reconversion 🔁

Total Failure

If all objects of conversion fail (all beneficiaries predecease the testator; perpetuity violation; illegality) — NO conversion. Property reverts in its original, unconverted form.

Partial Failure — Will (Ackroyd v. Smithson)

Property passes to the person entitled in its unconverted state — but they take it in its converted form. The realty beneficiary gets it (as it was originally land) but takes it as personalty.

Reconversion

The absolute owner may elect to reverse the notional conversion (Harcourt v. Seymour — equity does nothing in vain). Also occurs automatically by operation of law when the duty to convert and the right to the property vest in the same person.

Unit 8

Specific Performance

When money just won't cut it — equity orders you to actually do what you promised.

What Is Specific Performance? ⚡

A court order compelling exact performance of a contractual obligation — precisely as agreed. Equitable, discretionary, and an alternative (not addition) to damages. In Ibekwe v. Nwosu: requires "exact performance of a contract in the specific form in which it was made, where monetary damages cannot compensate the aggrieved party." The golden rule: you must demonstrate that damages are inadequate before a court will even consider granting it.

4 Core Features

Valid, subsisting, executory contract · Damages inadequate · Discretionary · Executory contracts only

🏗️
BFI Group Corporation v. BPEBinding Contract + Unilateral Change = Specific Performance Granted — Locus Classicus
⭐ Locus Classicus
📖 The Scenario

BFI's bid of US$410 million for ALSCON was declared the preferred bid. BPE then unilaterally changed the payment terms — requiring the 10% bid deposit within 15 days of a new letter instead of 15 days from signing the share purchase agreement. BFI failed the new (unauthorised) deadline. BPE tried to walk away.

⚖️ Held (Supreme Court)

There was a valid contract. BPE could not unilaterally alter agreed terms. The Supreme Court granted specific performance in favour of BFI.

WHEN GRANTED ✅ / WHEN REFUSED ❌

When Granted

Sale of Land (Most Common)

Brett JSC in Fakoya v. ALR: damages are "less often a complete remedy for breach of a contract for the sale of land" — land is unique. Plaintiff must show all conditions precedent fulfilled or readiness and willingness to perform (Biyo v. Aku).

Building Contracts (Wolverhampton Corp v. Emmons)

Four conditions: (1) work clearly defined; (2) plaintiff has special interest; (3) damages inadequate; (4) defendant has possession of the land.

Separation Deeds

Wilson v. Wilson: separation agreements are not contrary to public policy and can be specifically enforced. BUT Cartwright v. Cartwright: contracts providing for FUTURE separation are void.

The 8 Grounds for Refusal

1. Hardship

Severe hardship to the defendant (Ngwu v. Nnaji).

2. Illegal or Immoral Contracts

Clean hands maxim applies.

3. No Consideration (Even Under Deeds)

Equity will not assist a volunteer — even under a deed (Cannon v. Hartley; Oba Akenzua v. Benin Divisional Council — past consideration).

4. Personal Service Contracts

Cannot compel an opera singer to sing (CH Giles & Co. v. Morris); employment contracts (Afrotech Tech Services v. MIA & Sons SC).

5. Pure Money Obligations

Not available for contracts simply to pay a sum of money (South African Territories v. Wallington).

6–8. Insubstantial Interests / Requires Constant Supervision / Not Mutually Binding

Ryan v. Mutual Tontine Westminster Chamber Association (porter for block of flats — court cannot monitor daily compliance).

🏡
MBA-Ede v. OkufoPart Performance Defeats the "No Writing" Defence
📖 The Scenario

The plaintiff orally agreed to sell his house for ₦40,000. The defendant paid ₦19,000 and took possession by installing tenants. Defendant counter-claimed for specific performance of the oral contract.

⚖️ Held

Although the contract was unwritten, equity will not allow the statute to be used as an instrument of fraud. Since there was part performance (payment + possession + tenants), specific performance was ordered in favour of the defendant.

Unit 9

Injunctions

Stop what you're doing — or start doing it. Equity's most powerful day-to-day weapon.

What Is an Injunction? 🚫

An order of an equitable nature that either restrains a person from a specified act (prohibitory/restrictive) or, in exceptional cases, requires them to perform a specified act (mandatory/positive). Aimed at preserving the res or maintaining the status quo. Must be grounded in a recognised legal or equitable wrong — not mere annoyance (Day v. Browning vs. Street v. Union Bank of Spain & England).

American Cyanamid Co. v. Ethicon Ltd. — The Modern Test (adopted in Nigeria: Obeya Memorial Hospital v. AG Federation)

4-Step Test for Interlocutory Injunctions (Oyeyemi v. Irewole Local Government)

Replaces the old "strong prima facie case" test from Ladunni v. Kukoyi

Step 1 — Serious Question to Be Tried

Is the claim frivolous or vexatious? If not — there is a genuine issue. The court does NOT examine the merits at this stage. A serious question means there is a res that needs to be preserved.

Step 2 — Adequacy of Damages

Would an award of damages adequately compensate the plaintiff for pretrial loss? If yes — no injunction needed. If no — proceed.

Step 3 — Balance of Convenience

Who would suffer more significant, irreparable, or hard-to-compensate damage? (Tobi JCA in ACB v. Awogboro).

Step 4 — Undertaking as to Damages

The court extracts an undertaking as to damages — applicant's promise to pay if the injunction turns out to have been wrongly granted (Kotoye v. CBN).

Interim vs. Interlocutory — Classic Distinction (Kotoye v. CBN) 🆚

FeatureInterimInterlocutory
NoticeEx-parte — no notice to other partyMotion on notice — both parties heard
Duration7–14 days, until named dateUntil final determination of suit
TriggerExtreme, non-self-induced urgencySerious question + inadequate damages + balance of convenience

7 Grounds to Set Aside an Interim Injunction (A.G. Leventis v. Okongwu) ⚠️

  1. Failure to make full disclosure of all material facts (Thermax v. Schott Industrial Gas Ltd)
  2. Misrepresentation of facts to the court
  3. Irregularly granted — where it was actually possible to serve the other party
  4. Plaintiff failed to attend court for cross-examination
  5. Delay in bringing the application
  6. Delay in signing the undertaking as to damages
  7. Deliberate delay in hearing the motion on notice for interlocutory injunction
SPECIAL TYPES 🌟
Quia Timet — "Because He Fears"

4. Quia Timet Injunction

Your legal rights have not yet been infringed — but you have a well-founded fear that they are about to be. Equity intervenes before the wrong happens. Lord Upjohn in Redland Bricks Ltd v. Morris.

Named after Mareva Compania Naviera SA v. International Bulk Carriers SA (1975)

5. Mareva Injunction (Freezing Order)

Restrains a defendant from disposing of assets within jurisdiction to frustrate any eventual judgment. 7 conditions (Sotuminu v. Ocean Steamship): pending action; arguable case; assets in jurisdiction; imminent danger of dissipation; full disclosure; balance of convenience; undertaking as to damages.

6. Anton Piller Order (Search and Inspect)

Allows plaintiff to enter defendant's premises unannounced to inspect and preserve evidence. Granted ex-parte to prevent destruction of evidence. Authorised under Order 33 Rules 1 & 2, Uniform Civil Procedure Rules.

Ex-Parte & Fair Hearing 🔇

In 7-Up Bottling Co. Ltd v. Abiola & Sons Ltd (SC), the Supreme Court rejected the argument that ex-parte orders violate fair hearing — the emphasis is on "hearing within a reasonable time" (Deduwa v. Okorodudu). Full disclosure in the affidavit is mandatory — any concealment is fatal to the order.

Unit 10

Rescission, Rectification & Delivery Up

Setting aside contracts, correcting badly written documents, and forcing fraudulent deeds out of circulation.

What Is Rescission? 🔙

Rescission is an equitable remedy that allows a party to set aside (cancel) a transaction or contract. Importantly, it is primarily an act taken by the injured party — the court's order simply legally recognises the party's act of rescinding.

Two procedural requirements: (1) Notice — the rescinding party must inform the other party (unless communication has been made impossible); and (2) Court jurisdiction — needed to ensure restitutio in integrum (restoring both parties to their pre-contract positions) and to prevent the other party from later enforcing the cancelled agreement.

Grounds for Rescission 📋

  1. Misrepresentation — fraudulent or innocent
  2. Constructive Fraud / Undue Influence
  3. Contracts Uberrimae Fidei (utmost good faith)
  4. Misdescription
  5. Mistake
GROUND 1 — MISREPRESENTATION 🎭

Requirements for a Misrepresentation 📝

Four Requirements

For a statement to count as an actionable misrepresentation: (1) the statement was false; (2) it was a statement of fact (not mere opinion, generally); (3) it was addressed to the representee before or at the time the contract was entered into; and (4) it was intended to induce, and did in fact induce, the representee to enter into the contract.

1A. Fraudulent Misrepresentation

A deliberate attempt to deceive. The representor is strictly liable — even if the representee could have investigated the truth themselves.

Fraudulent misrepresentation makes a contract voidable. There must be actual fraud, and the false statement must have directly induced the plaintiff to act. The law holds the representor strictly liable — even if the representee did not rely on the misrepresentation or had the opportunity to investigate. Why? Because fraudulent misrepresentation is a deliberate attempt to deceive — the wrongdoer cannot escape by pointing to the victim's failure to double-check the lie.

🏠
Sule v. AromireFraud — "Could Have Found Out the Truth" Is No Defence
📖 The Scenario

A buyer purchased land at auction based on a false advertisement by the seller. He obtained the deed but couldn't take possession because the real owners stopped him. The seller argued: "The buyer could have found out the truth if he'd just investigated."

⚖️ Held

Ruled in favour of the buyer — annulment and refund. "The buyer could have found out the truth" is NOT a valid defence against fraud. Where a person is misled by fraud, the defrauding party cannot escape by pointing to the victim's failure to investigate.

⚠️
Williams v. AromireLimitation — Rescission Cannot Be Based on Mere Allegation
⚖️ Held

Rescission will not be granted based on a mere allegation of a defect in a property title — it must be proven. This case limited the rule in Sule v. Aromire.

🏨
Smith v. Land and House Property CorporationOpinion That Couldn't Honestly Be Held = Misrepresentation
📖 The Scenario

A vendor selling a hotel described the existing tenant as "a most desirable tenant." In reality, the tenant had been owing many months of rent arrears. The vendor claimed this was merely his opinion.

⚖️ Held

Although expressed as an opinion, the vendor could not honestly have held such an opinion. The statement was treated as a misrepresentation. The purchaser was allowed to rescind.

1B. Innocent Misrepresentation

No deceit — just a genuinely false statement. Common Law was strict; Equity intervened to be more generous.

At Common Law, innocent misrepresentation only provides relief if the false statement was actually written into the contract. Equity intervened and allows a party to rescind for innocent misrepresentation even if the false statement was NOT officially written into the contract. Exception for sale of goods: a party cannot rescind once they have legally "accepted" the goods.

Nigerian law note: While England amended its laws in 1967 to allow damages for innocent misrepresentation, Nigerian law still follows the older, stricter English position — damages cannot be awarded for innocent misrepresentation. J.O. Fabunmi suggests this needs updating. Equity only allows for rescission or refusing specific performance.

🥇
Ajayi v. EberuAcceptance of Goods Bars Rescission
📖 The Scenario

The plaintiff sold gold to the defendant. The defendant resold it to third parties. The gold turned out to be fake and was returned to the defendant. The defendant then refused to pay the plaintiff, purporting to rescind the original contract.

⚖️ Held

Ruled for the plaintiff (seller). By reselling the gold to third parties, the defendant performed an act inconsistent with the seller's ownership. Under the Sale of Goods Law, reselling constitutes "acceptance" of the goods — the right to rescind for innocent misrepresentation was gone. The defendant had to pay.

GROUND 2 — CONSTRUCTIVE FRAUD & UNDUE INFLUENCE 🤝

2. Constructive Fraud / Undue Influence

A transaction obtained through fraud, duress, or undue influence can be set aside. Equity ensures contracts are entered into freely — balancing this with the sanctity of contracts.

👨‍👧
Taylor v. BrewFather/Lawyer's Undue Influence Over Young Heiress
📖 The Scenario

A young woman inherited a fortune. Her father, also acting as her lawyer, convinced her to put the property in a trust and secretly added a clause stating that if she died without a will, he would get everything — cutting out her husband and mother's family. She married shortly after and died without a will. Her husband sued.

⚖️ Held

The trust deed was cancelled. The young woman did not understand what she was doing, and the deed was executed due to the undue influence of her father/lawyer. The concealed self-serving clause combined with her youth and dependence on his advice made the deed voidable.

🩺
Johnson v. WilliamsQuasi-Mother/Son Relationship — Presumption Created but Rebutted
📖 The Scenario

A sick woman gave property to her medical adviser. She later claimed undue influence. The Court of Appeal characterised their relationship as "quasi-mother and son."

⚖️ Held

A "quasi-mother and son" relationship creates a presumption of undue influence. However, the doctor rebutted the presumption by proving the gift was made freely after the donor fully understood the transaction. Presumption of undue influence can be displaced by showing the transaction was genuinely voluntary.

⚖️
Williams v. FranklinSolicitor Buying from Client — Independent Advice Not Absolute
📖 The Scenario

A solicitor bought land from his client after it failed at auction. Two of the client's relatives witnessed the sale. Someone later tried to cancel the sale, arguing the client had no independent legal advice.

⚖️ Held

The sale was upheld. Independent legal advice is not an absolute requirement if the sale can otherwise be proven to have been fair. Here: two relatives witnessed the transaction and a fair price was paid — sufficient evidence the client acted independently.

GROUND 3 — CONTRACTS UBERRIMAE FIDEI 🤫
Uberrimae Fidei — "Of Utmost Good Faith"

3. Contracts of Utmost Good Faith

One party has all the knowledge. They are legally required to make full disclosure of all material facts. Failure to do so — even innocently — allows the other party to rescind.

In these specific types of contracts, one party holds all the relevant information and the other party must rely entirely on what is disclosed. The law imposes a positive duty of full disclosure. Examples: insurance contracts, family settlements, and fiduciary relationships (lawyer/client, doctor/patient, trustee/beneficiary).

🪨
Tate v. WilliamsonTutor Conceals Mineral Rights from Student — Constructive Fraud
📖 The Scenario

An Oxford undergraduate in financial difficulties sought his tutor's advice. The tutor advised him to sell land he owned — and offered to buy it himself. The tutor failed to disclose that the land contained valuable minerals, which would have greatly enhanced its market value, and bought it for half what it was really worth. The student drank himself to death at age 24. His executors challenged the transaction.

⚖️ Held

The tutor was guilty of constructive fraud — the adviser/advisee relationship imposed a duty of full disclosure. The agreement was voidable at the instance of the executors. The tutor exploited his position of trust and the student's ignorance to benefit from an undisclosed material fact.

🚗
Century Insurance Co. Ltd v. AtumiyaHonest Belief Saves the Insurance Contract
📖 The Scenario

A man applied for car insurance and answered "No" when asked if any previous policy had ever been cancelled — genuinely not knowing his old policy had been cancelled. He had an accident, and the insurance company refused to pay, citing misrepresentation.

⚖️ Held

Ruled in favour of the insured. In insurance proposals asking for a person's belief, the contract cannot be voided if the person answered honestly and reasonably based on what they genuinely believed to be true — even if that belief turned out to be factually incorrect. The duty of utmost good faith requires honest disclosure, not omniscience.

GROUND 4 — MISDESCRIPTION 📏

4. Misdescription

If a seller substantially misdescribes what they are selling — whether directly or through an authorised agent like an auctioneer — the buyer can rescind. This counts as a material misrepresentation.

📐
Sodipo v. CokerLand Described as 22.2 Acres — Was Much Smaller
📖 The Scenario

A buyer purchased land at auction that was described as 22.2 acres. It turned out to be significantly smaller. The misdescription was made through the seller's authorised agent (the auctioneer).

⚖️ Held

The sale was set aside and the purchase price was refunded. A severe misdescription by an authorised agent is as binding on the seller as if the seller had made the description directly. The material difference between described and actual acreage justified rescission.

GROUND 5 — MISTAKE ❓

5. Mistake

A fundamental mistake — one that strikes at the very root of the agreement — can make a contract void ab initio. A less fundamental mistake makes it voidable.

Four Conditions for Equity to Intervene ✅

Conditions

(1) Equity will relieve a party from a mistake as long as it does not cause injustice to an innocent third party; (2) Both parties must be under a "common misapprehension" — the same fundamental mistake; (3) The mistake must be fundamental to the subject matter — not merely a mistake about quality; (4) The party asking to cancel must not be at fault for the mistake.

🕵️
Cundy v. LindsayMistake as to Identity — Contract Void from the Start
📖 The Scenario

A rogue pretended to be the owner of a well-known business (Blenkirn & Co.) and ordered goods from Lindsay. After receiving the goods, he resold them to Cundy (who had no idea of the fraud) and disappeared. Lindsay sued to recover the goods from Cundy.

⚖️ Held

The contract was vitiated by mistake as to identity. Lindsay intended to sell to Blenkirn & Co. — not the rogue. No title passed to the rogue, so the rogue could not pass valid title to Cundy. Cundy had to return the goods — losing both money (to the rogue) and goods (to Lindsay).

Mistake of Quality vs. Subject Matter 🔍

The mistake must be about the fundamental subject matter — not merely the quality of what is being exchanged. In Lever Brothers Ltd. v. Bell and Snelling, a company paid executives to leave without realising those executives had secretly breached their contracts (which would have allowed the company to fire them for free). The court refused to void the settlement. The mistake was about the quality of the executives' service — not the subject matter of the severance contract. The executives had no duty to confess their breaches.

Mistake of Fact vs. Mistake of Law 📚

Mistake of Fact

Money paid under a mistake of fact CAN generally be recovered. In Abraham v. Tijani, a man bought land twice (doubting his first title), then discovered his first title was actually good. He successfully recovered the money paid at the second (mistaken) auction.

Mistake of Law

Money paid under a mistake of law CANNOT be recovered. In Knight, Frank and Rutley v. AG Kano State, both parties failed to realise only local governments had power to levy tenement rates. Contract held void — the ₦100,000 advance could not be recovered.

LOSS OF THE RIGHT TO RESCIND ⏳

The Right to Rescind Can Be Lost — Four Ways

Rescission is not an absolute right. Act promptly — or lose it forever.

1. Delay / Acquiescence

A party must act promptly. Waiting too long implies acceptance of the situation — it is treated as an implied waiver.

2. Waiver by Conduct

If you act in a way that confirms the contract after discovering the issue — for example, continuing to perform or accepting benefits — you lose the right to cancel.

3. Impossibility of Restitutio in Integrum

If it is impossible to restore both parties to their original pre-contract positions, rescission will not be ordered. The purpose of rescission is to undo the transaction entirely.

4. Third-Party Rights

If an innocent third party has already acquired legal rights or interest in the property (e.g. a bona fide purchaser for value without notice), rescission cannot be ordered against them.

🖼️
Leaf v. International GalleriesFive-Year Delay = Right to Rescind Lost
📖 The Scenario

X purchased a painting in the belief — as asserted by the gallery — that it was by a famous master. Five years later, when X tried to sell it, he discovered it was a cheap imitation and virtually worthless. He sued for rescission.

⚖️ Held

His action was unsuccessful. By keeping the painting for five years, X had lost his right to reject it. The lengthy delay implied acceptance of the contract. Equity requires prompt action — you cannot sit on a right to rescind indefinitely.

🔨
Taiwo v. PrincewellContinuing to Work = Waiver by Conduct
📖 The Scenario

A builder discovered a plan was defective, agreed to a fee for unused work — but then kept building and accepting regular payments without securing that extra fee. He later tried to rescind the contract over the unpaid amount.

⚖️ Held

By continuing to work and accepting payment after discovering the defect, the builder had waived his right to rescind. Conduct inconsistent with an intention to cancel the contract destroys the right to cancel.

DELIVERY UP & CANCELLATION OF DOCUMENTS 📄

Delivery Up and Cancellation

A court order forcing a party to hand over a void or voidable document so it can be officially cancelled — preventing future fraud or deception of innocent third parties.

When Is Delivery Up Ordered? 📋

The remedy prevents someone from using a fraudulent, void, or voidable document to make a false claim or deceive innocent third parties who might later deal with the property. However, delivery up will NOT be ordered where a document is completely and obviously invalid just from looking at it (void on its face) — because there is no real risk of anyone being fooled by it. Most commonly granted where a document was obtained by fraud, duress, or undue influence.

🏗️
Erhumwunse v. OmoregbeDelivery Up Refused — No Sufficient Proof of Duress
📖 The Scenario

A man claimed he was forced into a loan agreement under duress and sought cancellation of the loan document. However, he had voluntarily given his house plans as security for the loan.

⚖️ Held

The court refused to cancel the document. The voluntary handing over of house plans as security was inconsistent with a claim of duress. A party cannot claim they were forced into an agreement while simultaneously performing voluntary acts under it.

🏦
Barclays Bank v. OlofintuyiFraudulent Sale Document Cancelled to Protect Bank
📖 The Scenario

A family representative mortgaged family land to Barclays Bank. He then fraudulently sold the same (already-mortgaged) land to a third party.

⚖️ Held

The court cancelled the fraudulent sale document to protect the bank's mortgage rights. A fraudulently obtained document affecting property that is already encumbered will be ordered to be delivered up and cancelled — to prevent it from defeating the prior legitimate interest.

📜
Adagun v. FagbolaUnauthorised Family Mortgage Cancelled
📖 The Scenario

A family member mortgaged property he did not actually have the right to sell or mortgage. The family challenged the mortgage deed.

⚖️ Held

The court cancelled the mortgage deed at the family's request. A person cannot mortgage property he has no legal authority to deal with. Delivery up and cancellation corrects the record where such an unauthorised dealing has been memorialised in a document.

RECTIFICATION ✏️

Rectification

The court corrects errors in a written document so that it accurately reflects the actual oral agreement made. The court does NOT fix bad contracts — only badly written documents. The mistake must have happened during the process of putting the oral agreement into writing.

Conditions Required for Rectification ✅

1. A True Prior Oral Agreement or Common Continuing Intention

There must have been a genuine oral agreement — or at minimum a "common continuing intention" — between the parties before they attempted to reduce it to writing.

2. The Mistake in Writing Must Be Mutual

Both parties must have been unaware of the error in the document. A unilateral mistake (only one party got it wrong) does not attract rectification — unless the other party committed fraud.

3. Who Can Apply?

Generally, only parties to the contract can seek rectification. However, for non-contractual documents (like a trust deed), a person with a strong interest — such as a beneficiary — can apply to the court for this remedy.

🏗️
Oyadiran v. BaggettErroneous Restriction Removed from Sublease
📖 The Scenario

A sublease was drafted and executed. It mistakenly included a heavy restriction about building industrial units — a restriction that neither party had actually agreed to in their oral negotiations. The sublessee sought rectification to have the restriction removed.

⚖️ Held

The court ordered the sublease to be rectified — the mistakenly included restriction was removed. Since neither party had agreed to it and it was a mutual error in drafting, equity corrected the written document to reflect the true oral agreement.

🎲 MASTER HYPOTHETICALS
🎭 Fraudulent Misrepresentation — No Excuse for "Could Have Investigated"

Adaeze sells a warehouse to Emeka, describing it as "fully compliant with all safety regulations." It is not — and Adaeze knows this. Emeka, busy, doesn't commission an independent survey. When the building fails a government inspection, Emeka sues for rescission. Adaeze says: "Emeka should have checked himself." Result: Rescission granted. The argument that the buyer could have found out the truth is NOT a valid defence against fraud. Sule v. Aromire applied.

🥇 Innocent Misrepresentation + Acceptance of Goods

Bola sells Tunde a batch of silver jewellery, genuinely believing it is solid silver. Tunde sells half of it at a market before discovering it is silver-plated (worth far less). He then tries to rescind the contract with Bola. Result: Rescission refused. By selling half the goods to third parties, Tunde "accepted" the goods — an act inconsistent with the seller's ownership. Once accepted, the right to rescind for innocent misrepresentation is extinguished: Ajayi v. Eberu.

🤫 Uberrimae Fidei — Insurance Honest Belief

Chidi applies for life insurance. The form asks: "Have you been diagnosed with any serious illness in the last 5 years?" Chidi answers "No" — genuinely not knowing that a blood test two years ago had flagged a pre-diabetic condition he was never told about. Chidi dies in an accident. The insurance company tries to void the policy for non-disclosure. Result: Policy stands. Chidi answered honestly and reasonably based on what he knew. The duty of utmost good faith requires honest disclosure — not disclosure of what you don't know. Century Insurance Co. v. Atumiya applied.

⏳ Loss of Right to Rescind — Delay + Conduct

Emeka buys a factory building from Tunde. Tunde falsely tells him it has planning permission for industrial use. Emeka discovers the truth immediately but then continues using the building for six months, sublets it to a third party, and only then tries to rescind. Result: Right to rescind is LOST. (1) Delay; (2) Waiver by conduct — continued use + subletting; (3) Third-party rights — the subtenant. Emeka's only remedy is damages for the misrepresentation. Leaf v. International Galleries; Taiwo v. Princewell applied.

✏️ Rectification — Correcting the Written Record

Ngozi and Kunle orally agree that Ngozi will lease a commercial space to Kunle for 5 years at ₦500,000 per year, with a right to renew for a further 5 years. When the lease is typed up, the typist accidentally omits the renewal clause entirely. Both parties sign without noticing. A year later, Ngozi claims there is no renewal right. Result: Rectification ordered. There was a true prior oral agreement including the renewal clause; the mistake was mutual; the written document simply failed to capture the true agreement. The court corrects the written lease to include the renewal clause. Oyadiran v. Baggett principle applied.

📄 Delivery Up — Fraudulent Document Cancelled

Alhaji has a valid registered title to a plot. His cousin forges a transfer document in his name and purports to sell the land to a developer. Alhaji discovers the forged document and applies for delivery up and cancellation. Result: Delivery up ordered. The forged deed was obtained by fraud and, left in circulation, could be used to deceive future purchasers. The court orders its cancellation. Barclays Bank v. Olofintuyi principle applied.

Unit 11

Equitable Defences

When the plaintiff comes to court — equity checks their behaviour too. These are the weapons the defendant fires back.

What Are Equitable Defences? 🛡️

Equitable defences are affirmative defences asking the court to excuse an act because the party bringing the claim has itself acted inequitably. Originally only in courts of equity — now available in all civil cases. They flow from the maxims: "He who comes to equity must come with clean hands," "Equity does not aid the indolent," and so on.

Key distinction: a denial attacks the existence of the claimant's cause of action; a defence admits the cause of action may exist but argues the defendant should be excused regardless. Burden of proof rests on the party asserting the defence.

The "Polar Star" of Equity

1. Bona Fide Purchaser for Value Without Notice

The ultimate shield — a defendant who took legal rights honestly, paid value, and had no notice of a prior equitable interest takes FREE of that interest.

Three requirements: (1) Bona fide — acting honestly with no knowledge of prior equity; (2) Purchaser for value — money, money's worth, marriage consideration, or satisfaction of existing debt; (3) Without notice — no actual, constructive, or imputed notice. James LJ in Pilcher v. Rawlins: the bona fide purchaser has "an absolute, unqualified, unanswerable defence" to the jurisdiction of equity.

2. Consent

A beneficiary who consents to — or authorises — a breach of trust cannot afterwards complain about it.

Where a beneficiary gives fully informed consent to a trustee acting outside the terms of the trust, this operates as a defence. The rule in Saunders v. Vautier underlies this. But a beneficiary cannot unilaterally vary the trust's terms without the trustee's agreement — variation needs the trustee's consent just as the original creation did.

Ex Turpi Causa Non Oritur Actio

3. Illegality & Unclean Hands

No action arises from a dishonourable cause. Two related but distinct doctrines.

Illegality

A person should not profit from their own wrongdoing. In Patel v. Mirza (UK SC), the old "reliance test" from Tinsley v. Milligan was rejected in favour of a "range of factors" approach — courts now weigh: seriousness of the illegality; connection to the claim; proportionality; public interest in deterrence. This approach now governs illegality in equity generally.

Unclean Hands

Focused on supererogatory (discretionary) remedies — specific performance, most injunctions. A claimant whose behaviour was inequitable in relation to the transaction will be denied these remedies. The doctrines operate on different tracks: illegality = preserving the law's legitimacy; unclean hands = whether awarding a discretionary remedy would encourage undesirable behaviour.

Vigilantibus non dormientibus jura subveniunt

4. Laches & Acquiescence

Equity aids the vigilant — not those who sleep on their rights. Delay + prejudice = your claim is barred.

Laches

Unreasonable delay in asserting rights — standing by/inaction while rights are being infringed. Operates as a waiver. Key test: length of delay + circumstances making enforcement inequitable (Gidado v. Lawal; Kayode v. Odutola).

Acquiescence

With full knowledge of rights and infringement, your silence/conduct implies consent. Operates by estoppel. Does not necessarily involve long delay — involves conduct suggestive of fraud (Ramsden v. Dyson; Adebo v. Omisola).

🏗️
Ramsden v. DysonLord Cranworth's Classic Acquiescence Statement — Locus Classicus
⭐ Locus Classicus
📖 Lord Cranworth LC

"If a stranger begins to build on my land supposing it to be his own, and I, perceiving his mistake, abstain from setting him right, and leave him to persevere in his error, a Court of equity will not allow me afterwards to assert my title to the land on which he had expended money on the supposition that the land was his own."

⚖️ The Rule

Silence in the face of another's detrimental reliance = equitable fraud. You cannot stand by, watch another build on your land, and then assert title after the money has been spent.

Three Justifications for the Plaintiff ⚡

i. Ignorance of Rights

If the plaintiff did not know of their own legal rights, they cannot be blamed for failing to assert them: Salako v. Dosunmu; Gidado v. Lawal.

ii. Defendant's Fraud

Where the defendant acted fraudulently, the defence cannot be sustained: Fagge v. Amadu.

iii. Legal Disability

Where the plaintiff was an infant/minor, mentally ill, or physically incapacitated at the time the relevant acts occurred, laches will not be sustained.

⚖️
Laches vs. Statute of Limitations

Laches only operates where there is NO applicable statutory limitation period. If a limitation statute applies (expressly or by analogy), equity follows the law — the plaintiff gets the full statutory period. Pure laches only steps in where no statute covers the claim.

From French "estouppail" — to stop up a hole

5. Estoppel

Something that stops a party from asserting facts or rights inconsistent with what they previously represented — where the other party has relied on that representation to their detriment.

Coke: "a man's own act or acceptance stoppeth or closeth his mouth to allege or plead the truth." There is no single doctrine of estoppel — Lord Denning called them "a big house with many rooms, each used differently." But all forms share a common thread: detrimental reliance on a representation or assurance. Lord Goff: "It is unconscionability which provides the link between them."

5A. Proprietary Estoppel — A Sword AND A Shield

Three elements (Re Basham): Assurance · Reliance · Detriment. Creates entirely new property rights — not merely protective.

Element A — Assurance or Representation

Defendant made a representation intended to lead the claimant to believe they would acquire rights in property. Must be serious — not idle hints. Robert Walker LJ: "some elderly persons derive enjoyment from dropping hints about their intentions, without any question of estoppel arising."

Element B — Reliance

Claimant must have relied on the representation — nexus between the acts performed and the representation made. The reliance must be reasonable in context.

Element C — Detriment

The claimant must have suffered some detriment. Detriment is construed broadly — not limited to spending money (Gillett v. Holt: different types of representation can connote different forms of detriment).

🏠
Pascoe v. TurnerProprietary Estoppel as a Sword — New Right Created
📖 The Scenario

A woman was promised she would be entitled to live in a house for the rest of her life. In reliance on that promise, she spent money decorating and improving the property. Her partner later tried to evict her. No formal legal document gave her any interest.

⚖️ Held

Proprietary estoppel established — assurance ✅, reliance ✅, detriment ✅. The only remedy which would truly protect her reliance was a transfer of the freehold interest in the property to her. Proprietary estoppel created an entirely new property right.

📜
Re BashamPromise of Inheritance + Acts of Detriment = New Right
📖 The Scenario

A stepdaughter was repeatedly assured she would inherit a house under her stepfather's will. In reliance, she performed numerous acts to her detriment — caring for the dying stepfather, neglecting her own affairs. The stepfather died intestate — the house would pass to his blood relatives under intestacy.

⚖️ Held

Proprietary estoppel passed the property to the stepdaughter over the intestacy rules. Edward Nugee QC: where one person has acted to their detriment on the basis of a belief known to and encouraged by another, that other person "cannot insist on his strict legal rights if to do so would be inconsistent with A's belief."

Central London Property Trust Ltd v. High Trees House Ltd (1947)

5B. Promissory Estoppel — A Shield Only

A clear promise, relied upon by the other party, cannot be reneged upon even without consideration. But it cannot create new rights.

Foundation: Hughes v. Metropolitan Railway. Modern doctrine: Central London Property Trust v. High Trees House Ltd (Lord Denning) — a wartime agreement to halve rent estopped the landlord from claiming the full rent during the war period.

Promissory Estoppel

Shield only — protects existing rights, cannot create new ones. Operates in contractual context. Does NOT replace consideration. Cannot be used as a sword.

Proprietary Estoppel

Sword AND shield — actively creates new rights in property. Can override formal statutory requirements (Yaxley v. Gotts). Full proprietary remedy available.

📜 History of Equity — In 10 Sentences

  • Common Law became rigid after 1066 — your problem had to fit a pre-existing writ or you got no remedy.
  • The Provisions of Oxford 1258 froze the creation of new writs. Westminster II 1285 gave a small window via in consimili casu.
  • Common Law judges frustrated everything, so people petitioned the King → petitions forwarded to the Chancellor.
  • Early Chancellors (clergymen) decided cases purely on conscience. John Selden mocked this as "the Chancellor's foot."
  • The Earl of Oxford's Case (1615): King James I sided with Chancery — equity prevails over Common Law when they conflict.
  • Lord Nottingham's era brought systematization: equity by precedent, not personal conscience.
  • Judicature Act 1873: equity prevails. Judicature Act 1875: one Supreme Court, both systems applied together.
  • Berry v Berry: after the Judicature Acts, a simple written agreement can override a deed (equity prevails).
  • Walsh v Lonsdale: equity treats an enforceable written agreement for a lease as good as the lease itself.
  • Nigeria received equity through the High Court Laws of each state and Section 45(1) Interpretation Act.

📋 The 12 Maxims — One-Liners

  • No wrong without remedy: Equity fills the gaps Common Law leaves. Trusts, specific performance, discovery — all from this.
  • Equity follows the law: No law, no equity. Equity supplements — it cannot create a legal estate out of thin air (Chidiak v Coker).
  • Equal equity → law prevails: Legal interest beats equitable where both parties are equally clean (Pilcher v Rawlins).
  • Equal equities → first in time: Earlier equitable interest wins (Cave v Cave; Assaf v Fuwa). Exception for personalty: Dearle v Hall (first to give notice wins).
  • Seek equity, do equity: Must be willing to act fairly to get relief — Lodge v National Union Investment. Future conduct test.
  • Come with clean hands: Your past conduct in THIS transaction must be clean. Craig v Craig; Gill v Lewis (misconduct must be connected).
  • Delay defeats equity: Laches — sleep on rights, lose them. Limitation periods: contracts 6yrs, land 12yrs, public land 20yrs.
  • Equality is equity: Divide equally where no fair basis for inequality. Equity prefers tenancy in common (no jus accrescendi in commerce).
  • Substance over form: Equity X-rays the transaction. A "promissory note" with a house pledged = mortgage.
  • Done as ought to be done: Obligatory future acts treated as already performed. Walsh v Lonsdale; Iragunima v Rivers State Housing Authority.
  • Intention to fulfill obligation: Equity imputes fulfillment between spouses and parent/child only. Does not perfect imperfect gifts.
  • In personam: Equity acts on the person. Global reach — injunctions can reach conduct anywhere as long as the defendant is in jurisdiction.

🏆 Priority — The Decision Tree

  • Legal v Legal: First in time wins. Nemo dat — once a grantor has given everything away, he has nothing left to give (Tewogbade v Obadina).
  • Legal v Equitable: Legal wins IF equities are equal (holder is BFPVWN). If the legal holder is guilty of gross negligence or fraud, equitable interest may win (Northern Counties v Whipp).
  • Equitable v Equitable: First in time wins — UNLESS the earlier holder's conduct (fraud, gross negligence, misleading documents) makes their equity less meritorious (Rice v Rice; Re King's Settlement).
  • Bona fide purchaser: Clean hands + valuable consideration + no notice + legal estate = absolute defence against prior equitable interests (Pilcher v Rawlins).
  • Pure personalty priority: Rule in Dearle v Hall — first to give notice to the trustee wins, not first to create the interest.

👁️ Notice + 📑 Registration — Quick Rules

  • Actual notice: Personal knowledge — however obtained, whenever obtained before completion. Must deal with it before completing (Jared v Clements).
  • Constructive notice: The "prudent man" test. Inspect the land (Animashaun v Olojo). Search the register. Investigate title. A rumour is enough to trigger the duty to inquire (Williamson v Brown).
  • Imputed notice: Your agent's actual and constructive notice = your knowledge — EXCEPT: knowledge from a previous, unrelated transaction cannot be imputed in a later transaction (Okunubi v Assaf).
  • Registration priority: Priority = date of registration, not date of creation (Amankra v Zankley; Orianzi v AG Rivers State). First to register = first in priority.
  • Registration does NOT cure defective title — nemo dat still applies (Ayinla v Sijuwola). Unregistered instrument cannot prove title but CAN prove payment + equitable interest.

📦 Assignment + 🔄 Conversion — Core Rules

  • Assignment vs. Novation: Assignment needs no debtor consent. Novation requires ALL parties' consent. Consent to novation can be inferred from conduct (G.B. Ollivant v. Effioms Transport).
  • Legal Assignment (NAWA NI): Notice (written, to debtor); Assignment (not mere direction); Writing; Absolute (entire chose, not partial, not by charge); No consideration required; Identity of assignee clear.
  • Equitable Assignment: No special form — only clear intention. Exceptions: subsisting trusts and copyright must be in writing.
  • Joinder Rules: Equitable chose + absolute = sue alone. Equitable chose + non-absolute = join assignor. Legal chose + equitable assignment = MUST join assignor (whether absolute or not).
  • Dearle v. Hall: Between competing equitable assignees, priority = date of notice to trustee/debtor, not date of creation.
  • Conversion: Equity treats land as money and money as land from the moment a binding intention to convert exists. Based on "equity regards as done that which ought to be done." HIID limits: Hardship, no Intention, Inequitable Results, Discretion.
  • 6 Circumstances: Binding contract for sale; option to purchase (Lawes v. Bennett); testamentary trust for sale (must be imperative); statutory trust for sale (PCL ss.61–64); partnership property; court order.

⚡ Specific Performance + 🚫 Injunctions — Core Rules

  • Specific Performance: Valid, subsisting, executory contract; damages inadequate; discretionary; executory contracts only. Granted most often for sale of land (land is unique), building contracts (4 conditions: Wolverhampton Corp v. Emmons), and separation deeds. Refused for personal service contracts (CH Giles; Afrotech v. MIA), pure money obligations, insubstantial interests, contracts requiring constant supervision. Part performance defeats "no writing" defence (MBA-Ede v. Okufo; Anaeze v. Anyoso).
  • Interlocutory Injunction Test (American Cyanamid, adopted in Nigeria: Obeya Memorial Hospital v. AG Fed.): (1) Serious question to be tried; (2) Damages inadequate; (3) Balance of convenience; (4) Undertaking as to damages. Old "strong prima facie case" test (Ladunni v. Kukoyi) REJECTED.
  • Interim vs. Interlocutory (Kotoye v. CBN): Interim = ex-parte, days only, extreme urgency. Interlocutory = both parties heard, lasts till final determination. NEVER use interchangeably in exam.
  • Special Types: Quia Timet — pre-emptive, no wrong yet (Redland Bricks v. Morris). Mareva — freezes assets within jurisdiction (7 conditions: Sotuminu v. Ocean Steamship). Anton Piller — search and inspect, ex-parte evidence preservation.

🔙 Rescission, Rectification & Delivery Up — Core Rules

  • Rescission: Set aside a contract — primarily the injured party's act, court order recognises it. Requirements: notice to the other party + court jurisdiction to ensure restitutio in integrum.
  • 5 Grounds: (1) Fraudulent misrepresentation — strictly liable even if representee could have investigated (Sule v. Aromire); "could have found out" is no defence. (2) Innocent misrepresentation — equity allows rescission even without writing, but not once goods are accepted (Ajayi v. Eberu); Nigerian law does NOT award damages for innocent misrepresentation. (3) Constructive fraud/undue influence — Taylor v. Brew; Johnson v. Williams (presumption can be rebutted); Williams v. Franklin (independent advice not absolute). (4) Contracts uberrimae fidei — one party has all knowledge, must fully disclose; Tate v. Williamson (constructive fraud); Century Insurance v. Atumiya (honest belief protects insured). (5) Misdescription — material misdescription by seller or authorised agent (Sodipo v. Coker). (6) Mistake — must be fundamental to subject matter, not quality (Cundy v. Lindsay — identity); mistake of fact recoverable (Abraham v. Tijani); mistake of law NOT recoverable (Knight, Frank and Rutley v. AG Kano State).
  • 4 Ways to Lose the Right to Rescind: Delay/acquiescence (Leaf v. International Galleries — 5-year delay); waiver by conduct (Taiwo v. Princewell — continuing to work); impossibility of restitutio in integrum; third-party rights acquired by bona fide purchaser.
  • Delivery Up: Court orders a party to hand over a void/voidable document for cancellation — to prevent future fraud. Refused if the document is void on its face (no real risk of deception). Refused where insufficient proof of duress (Erhumwunse v. Omoregbe). Granted for fraudulent dealings (Barclays Bank v. Olofintuyi; Adagun v. Fagbola).
  • Rectification: Court corrects a badly written document to match the true oral agreement — NOT a bad contract. Two conditions: (1) true prior oral agreement or common continuing intention; (2) mutual mistake in drafting. Unilateral mistake does NOT attract rectification (unless the other party committed fraud). Key case: Oyadiran v. Baggett.

🛡️ Equitable Defences — Core Rules

  • Bona Fide Purchaser (the "Polar Star"): Absolute, unanswerable defence (Pilcher v. Rawlins). Three requirements: bona fide; purchaser for value; without notice. Defeats all prior equitable interests.
  • Consent: Beneficiary with full knowledge who authorises a breach of trust cannot later sue. Saunders v. Vautier. But a beneficiary cannot unilaterally vary trust terms — trustee's consent needed.
  • Illegality & Unclean Hands: Ex turpi causa. Post Patel v. Mirza (UK SC): "range of factors" approach replaces reliance test. Unclean Hands: denied for supererogatory (discretionary) remedies where claimant's conduct was inequitable.
  • Laches: Substantial delay + inequitable circumstances = waiver (Kayode v. Odutola). Excused where: plaintiff ignorant of rights; defendant's fraud; legal disability. Operates only where no statutory limitation applies.
  • Acquiescence: With full knowledge of rights and infringement, plaintiff's silence/conduct implies consent (Ramsden v. Dyson; Nsiegbe v. Mgbemena). 5 elements (Laosebikan v. Awojobi): mistake; expenditure; plaintiff knows own right; plaintiff knows of defendant's mistake; plaintiff encouraged the expenditure.
  • Proprietary Estoppel (Sword AND Shield): Three elements (Re Basham): Assurance; Reliance; Detriment. Creates entirely new property rights (Pascoe v. Turner — freehold transferred; Re Basham — inheritance awarded). Remedy at court's discretion — from full transfer to monetary compensation (Baker v. Baker).
  • Promissory Estoppel (Shield Only): Foundation: Hughes v. Metropolitan Railway; modern doctrine: High Trees (Lord Denning). Clear promise + reliance + inequitability of resiling = estoppel. Cannot create new rights or replace consideration.
